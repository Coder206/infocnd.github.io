Ions complexes (radicaux)

Règles de la nomenclature

  1. Composés ioniques
    • Nomme l'élément négatif en premier et ajoute le suffixe "URE" (ex: NaCl = Chlorure de sodium
    • Ajoute le suffixe "YDE" pour les composés binaires formés d'oxygène (ex: MgO = Oxyde de magnésium)
    • Lorsque les composés ioniques sont formés d'atomes à deux valences, nomme l'élément négatif en premier et ensuite nomme l'élément positif suivi de la valence entre les parenthèses. (ex: CuCl2 = chlorure de cuivre (II))
  2. Composés formés d'ions polyatomiques
    • Nomme l'ion polyatomique en premier et ensuite nomme l'élément positif (ex: KNO2 = nitrite de potassium)
  3. Composés covalents
    • Ajoute les préfixes grecs (mono, di, tri, tetra, penta, hexa...) pour indiquer le nombre de chaque atome dans la molécule (ex: CO2 = dioxyde de carbone)
  4. Acides
    • Ajoute le suffixe "HYDRIQUE" aux acides binaires (ex: HCl = acide chlorhydrique)
    • Acides formés d'ions polyatomiques:
      • Quand l'ion polyatomique se termine en "ATE" l'acide prend le suffixe "IQUE"
      • Quand l'ion polyatomique se termine en "ITE" l'acide prend le suffixe "EUX"
  5. Bases
    • Le nom d'une base commence par hydroxyde (OH) et se termine par l'élément positif (le premier de la formule) (ex: NaOH = hydroxyde de sodium)

Sentences

A sentence almost always has three features
1. A subject
2. A verb
3. A complete thought

Three types of sentences will be presented here:
1. Simple Sentences
2. Compound Sentences
3. Complex Sentences

Simple Sentences:
A Simple sentence is one independent clause (IC).

Examples:
Mr. Shipley travelled to Canada last fall.

The old truck was pulled by a team of horses.

Bram rarely mowed the lawn.

Compound Sentences:
A Compound sentence is composed of two or more independent clauses (ICs).

Examples:
Mr. Shipley travelled to Canada last fall but he hadn't the money to do any sightseeing.

The old truck was pulled by a team of horses, so that john could save on the cost of fuel.

Bram rarely mowed the lawn, and he never fixed the fence poles either.

Problems frequently occur when two simple sentence (ICs) are being joined to make a compound sentence. These problems are called run-on sentences. These may have a period as its only punctuation mark OR it may be inappropriately punctuated with one or more commas (this type of run-on sentence is called a "comma splice").

Example:

John can't wait to go to the dance Arlene attends weekly.

Comma splices

Examples:
John can't wait to go to the dance Arlene attends weekly.

1. Use a period to separate the intendent clause into sentence.
2. Use a semicolon to separate the independent clauses within a single sentence.
3. Use a coordinating conjunction and a comma to join the two clauses Comma slices.

This is a comma splice:
John is going to the dance, Arlene attends every week.

This is how to fix it:
Use a semicolon, a transitional expression, and a comma.

John is going to the dance; likewise, Arlene is also going.

Hagar is a very wise woman; However, she is beginning to change her ways in old age.

Complex Sentences:

A Complex sentence is composed of one independent clause with one or more subordinate (dependent) clauses.

A subordinate (dependent) clause is of secondary importance in a sentence and depends on an independent clause to make a sentences.

Examples:
Because he was late for work, he had to work in the evening.

If we all pool our money, we ca buy you a university education.

Murray befriended Hagar who looked this and forlorn.

**Note the placement of a comma after the dependent clause.**

When the dependent clause is at the beginning of a sentence, the pattern is the following:
DC, IC (DEPENDENT CLAUSE, INDEPENDENT CLAUSE)

When the dependent clause is at the end of the sentence, there is no comma needed.
ID DC (independent clause dependent clause)

Sentence fragments:

A sentence fragment is a piece of information that is punctuated as a sentence but that lacks some of the characteristics of a complete sentence.

Example:
Tired of having to ask her for help.

The statement does not have a subject; we do not know who is tired of having to ask her to help.

How to fix it: add a subject.
Hagar is tired of having to ask her for help.

Tombstones in the Manawaka cemetery.

The statement does not contain a verb. Looked at by itself, we do not know what is being said about the tombstones in the cemetery.

How to fix it: add a verb.
There were people laying flowers on tombstones in the Manawaka cemetery.

I was late for church. Because Bram was drunk again.

The phrase is a dependent clause; it provides additional information about the independent clause. By itself, it does not provide a complete thought.

How to fix it: change the punctuation.
I was late for church, because Bram was drunk again.

OR

I was late for church. Because Bram was drunk again, I missed the reverend's sermon.
